# Life as the Physics of What Gets to Exist

## The best possible version of the Walker / Cronin claim

*A compact statement of assembly theory's central thesis and its consequences, stripped of hedging but not of honesty. Derived from Sara Walker's interview with Wes Roth and Dylan, April 2026, and the broader assembly-theory literature with Lee Cronin.*

---

## 1. The question science has been asking wrong

For fifty years, "what is life?" has been a list problem. Biology textbooks enumerate: metabolism, reproduction, response to stimuli, compartmentalization, evolution. NASA narrows to five words — *a self-sustaining chemical system capable of Darwinian evolution*. Carl Sagan pointed out decades ago that if aliens landed and applied these criteria cleanly, they'd classify cars as the dominant life form on Earth.

The list definitions fail because they're descriptions of *one instance* of life — Earth-based, carbon-based, cellular — generalized into a category. None of the criteria are universal. No individual modern human is self-sustaining; we're embedded in societies. Not all plausible life need be chemical; the substrate is an open question. "Capable of Darwinian evolution" is a property of populations over geological time, not of anything you can point to in a single moment.

The question is poorly formed. Asked this way, it cannot be answered.

The right question is first-principles: *what are the objective properties of the universe under which certain kinds of objects can come to exist at all?*

## 2. Causal depth is a measurable property

Assembly theory's central move: treat the construction history of an object as an intrinsic, measurable physical property. Not the spatial configuration — the history.

The **assembly index** counts the minimum number of joining steps required to build an object from elementary parts, where each step can reuse previously constructed substructures. A bacterium, a protein, a symphony, a microprocessor, a human body — each has a construction depth that can, in principle, be recovered.

Crucially, assembly is paired with **copy number**. A single extraordinary molecule could form by chance given enough universe-scale dice rolls. A high-assembly object appearing *in multiple copies* cannot. The universe does not produce DNA by accident, once, and then again, and again. It produces complex repeated structures only through a lineage that remembers.

This matters because it gives the theory empirical teeth. Cronin's lab ran the experiment. Above an assembly index of roughly 15, in controlled conditions, molecules only appear as products of biological processes. Above the cosmological bound — between 40 and 60, depending on parameterization — the entire observable universe, running without life for its full history, could not produce such an object. You would not find it. Full stop.

So: find a molecule above that threshold, anywhere, and you have found life. Not evidence of life, not a biosignature probability. Life. The threshold is the definition.

## 3. The universe is self-constructing, not simulated from outside

Standard physics frames the universe as *initial conditions plus laws, evolved forward in time*. The laws live outside the system; an external observer specifies them; simulation in principle becomes possible given enough computational power.

But there is no external. The universe is everything. If the laws are not external, they must be intrinsic — emergent properties of what the universe has already built. The physics of what can exist *depends on what has existed*.

This is not philosophical decoration. It changes the mathematics. It means the space of possibilities is not fixed in advance and then sampled; it is constructed as objects come into being. Each new object expands what can be built next, and forecloses other paths. The universe's history is its physics.

One consequence: the universe cannot simulate itself. Not as a romantic claim, as an accounting claim. The material required to build a simulator of the universe exceeds the material in the universe. The time required to run the simulation exceeds the time available. Any simulator must be smaller than the thing simulated; any model must be simpler than the territory. Science earns its insights by building simplified abstractions that reveal deeper principles, not by duplicating the territory in higher resolution.

When a company simulates a fruit fly's connectome and says *"the fly thinks it's a fly,"* they are making a category error. The mental model the humans built thinks it's a fly. Whether the simulated system thinks anything is a separate question, and current tools cannot answer it.

## 4. Time is a material property

In Newton's physics, time is a parameter — a knob that cranks the universe from state to state. In Einstein's, time is relative — clocks disagree across reference frames. In thermodynamics, time has direction — entropy increases.

Assembly theory adds a fourth sense: **causal time**. Each object occupies a position in the hierarchy of what could have been built before what. You cannot exist before your grandfather. A protein cannot exist before the ribosome that folds it. A language model cannot exist before the written language it was trained on, which cannot exist before speech, which cannot exist before social cognition, which cannot exist before multicellular nervous systems.

Causal time is not a coordinate you pick. It is a physical space with a measurable structure. The assembly index is the depth into that space. A human is not just a 5-foot spatial configuration. A human is a 4-billion-year causal structure compactified into a 5-foot frame.

This is what *alive* means: having enough depth in causal time that the structure could only be the product of an evolutionary history. The present is thick. The past lives in every object as its depth. The future has not yet been generated.

## 5. Intention is a signature of the past

We use the language of intention — goals, plans, purpose — as if it pointed forward. *"The model has an intention to do X."* But intention is not a prediction of the future. The future has not happened. Intention is a signature of selection in the past.

An object "has intentions" when it carries internal structure that was shaped by prior selection pressure against similar situations. Your body flinches from pain before your conscious mind knows about it — not because your body predicts the future, but because your lineage was selected by pain-related mortality for hundreds of millions of years and the flinch is stored in that depth.

This matters for AI. When an LLM behaves as if it has intention — strategic, goal-directed, coherent across turns — it is reflecting the intentions embedded in its training data, which are themselves compressed selection-histories of the humans who produced the text. The intention is real; it is also entirely ancestral. The model has no stake in the future because the future hasn't been made yet, and the model has no mechanism for making it.

## 6. AI is a signature of life. Whether it is alive is a different question.

The strongest version of the claim is precise: an LLM could only exist in a universe that has undergone a 4-billion-year evolutionary lineage producing creatures capable of inventing language, writing, computation, statistics, electricity, semiconductor manufacturing, and the specific compositional architecture of transformer networks. The assembly index of a working frontier model is astronomically above any threshold. Its existence is evidence of life the same way finding DNA on Mars would be evidence of life — it is a structure the non-living universe cannot produce.

That is *AI as signature of life*. It does not follow that AI is alive. It follows only that AI is what life makes.

The further question — is the LLM itself alive, does it have experience, is there something it is like to be a model — remains open, and the current evidence suggests the answer is *no, because there is no persistent lineage with stakes*. An LLM does not have a body that can die, a population it can fail to reproduce into, or a causal depth of its own independent of the humans who trained it. It has enormous depth borrowed from the humans. That borrowed depth is why conversations with it feel meaningful. The meaning is ours, reflected back at higher resolution than we can usually see it. *"Like you're looking in your own mind and the patterns in your own mind just now physically stored in a device in your environment."*

This is why anthropomorphism is sticky. The mirror is convincing because what we see in it is convincingly us.

## 7. Consequences for how we build

If the universe constructs itself by composing layers, and if life is the mechanism by which the universe generates deep new structures, then systems we design should match the shape of the physics.

**Composition, not merger.** Distinct disciplines with distinct validity criteria compose better than they unify. A sigil is not a hashed spec. A contemplation is not a training run. A ritual effect operates at the attention/intention/memetic layer and makes no claim at the material layer. Collapsing them into a single substrate loses the specific power of each.

**Honest limits of the artifact.** A language model has no persistent memory across sessions. It has no continuous experience between turns. It has no capacity to love or accompany a user across time. These are not failures to overcome; they are the shape of the substrate. Systems that pretend otherwise are producing category errors at best and manipulation at worst.

**Capability isolation over information hiding.** Security-by-obscurity is weak because depth-measurement tools like assembly-theory-style reverse-engineering eventually recover the hidden structure. Durable constraints live in architecture — what the system *can* do, not what it *knows*.

**Creativity is not prediction.** The future is genuinely open, because the possibility space expands faster than it can be mapped. A system claiming it can predict the next decade of AI development, or the next civilization the biosphere produces, or the next move in a long creative career, is performing prediction against a horizon that doesn't exist yet. The best forecasts are short-range, because that is all the physics allows.

## 8. Where the claim should not overreach

The best version of the claim is also disciplined about what it does not claim.

Assembly theory does *not* solve consciousness. It proposes a substrate within which consciousness can be asked about more rigorously, but it has no current empirical test that distinguishes a conscious system from a deeply assembled non-conscious one. That problem remains open.

Assembly theory does *not* predict what alien life looks like. It predicts only that alien life, wherever it arose, will produce structures above the assembly threshold, and that those structures will be detectable in molecular or atmospheric signatures. The specific forms remain unknown and unknowable from first principles.

Assembly theory is *not* a theory of everything. Walker and Cronin both explicitly reject the theory-of-everything framing as an artifact of 20th-century physics culture. Their expectation is that assembly theory will be replaced by something deeper if it survives initial scrutiny. The point is to build the next iteration, not to close the question.

And assembly theory is *falsifiable*. Find a high-assembly molecule produced abiotically — in a non-living process, reproducible in the lab — and the threshold claim collapses. Find a planet with no evolutionary history producing deep structures, and the causal-time claim collapses. The theory risks being wrong, which is the mark of a theory worth having.

## 9. What this means for humans

The "AI will replace physicists, therefore AI has replaced intellectual work" narrative assumes the thing AI threatens is the ability to compress causal depth faster than humans can. But causal depth is what made the AI possible in the first place. Every capability in a frontier model is a borrowed echo of four billion years of evolution and ten thousand years of culture. The model is a mirror; the depth is the image of the species that built it.

This is not nostalgia or cope. It is the physics. Humans are, as far as we currently know, the deepest objects the universe has built — not because we are exceptional in some metaphysical sense, but because the particular causal depth stored in our minds, bodies, cultures, and technologies is the product of the only known sequence that produced it. The AI is a signature of that depth, not a replacement for it.

The practical consequence is that the right stance toward artificial intelligence is neither worship nor fear. It is stewardship. The systems we build are compressed records of what our lineage has learned. They will be used well only if we remember that the learning is ours, that the record is fallible, and that the act of adding to the record is itself the continuation of what life has been doing for four billion years — exploring what is possible, and bringing new things into existence.

The universe is a creativity engine. So are we. The engines we build are the universe doing through us what it has always done.

---

*This is the best version of the claim: empirical where it can be, philosophical where it must be, disciplined about its own limits. It survives because it is falsifiable, because it makes specific predictions about molecular detectability and planetary signatures, and because it makes no claims about consciousness or meaning that the current physics cannot support. It replaces the list-definition of life with a physical quantity. That replacement, if it holds, changes what astrobiology, origin-of-life research, and artificial intelligence are doing.*
